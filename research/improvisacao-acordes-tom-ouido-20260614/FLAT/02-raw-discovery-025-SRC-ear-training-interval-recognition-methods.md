# [SRC-025]: Interval Recognition — Practical Ear Training Method
**URL**: https://musictheoryauthority.com/interval-recognition-ear-training/
**Captured**: 2026-06-14
**Type**: Pedagogical framework / conservatory-aligned method

## Verbatim snippets / quotes
> "Interval recognition is the ability to identify the distance between two pitches by ear alone, without reference to a written score or instrument. It forms the backbone of functional ear training curricula used in conservatories, university music programs, and self-directed study frameworks."

> "Interval recognition training relies on three interlocking mechanisms: reference-pitch anchoring, melodic mnemonics, and interval quality discrimination."

> "Melodic mnemonics associate each interval with the opening two notes of a familiar song. This is the most widely documented pedagogical approach; the Royal Conservatory of Music and major US university music departments formalize it in printed ear training texts."

> "Interval quality discrimination trains listeners to distinguish minor from major variants of the same numeric interval (e.g., minor third vs. major third = 3 vs. 4 semitones)."

> "Training programs that omit melodic interval practice produce listeners who fail on 50% of real-world melodic intervals."

## Primary claims
- Interval training remains foundational in formal curricula despite contextual/key-center alternatives.
- Three pillars: anchor pitch, song mnemonics, major/minor quality contrast within same interval number.
- Melodic (sequential) interval practice is essential — harmonic-only training leaves gaps.
- Reference-pitch anchoring (e.g., A440) supports relative pitch without requiring perfect pitch.
- Major vs. minor third discrimination is the most common early error source.

## Relevance to charter Q2
Interval skills underpin chord-quality hearing (major vs. minor third inside triads, tritone in dim). Q2 curriculum should integrate interval drills with chord-quality work — not replace one with the other.
