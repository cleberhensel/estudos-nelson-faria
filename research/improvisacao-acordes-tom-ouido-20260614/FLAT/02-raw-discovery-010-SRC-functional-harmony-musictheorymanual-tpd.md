## Verbatim snippets / quotes
> In the diagram below, with viio going to I, the chords start to divide themselves into three groups or functions: **Tonic:** vi and I; **Pre-dominant:** ii(7) and IV; **Dominant:** V(7) and viio.

> Tonic chords are those chords that are at rest – I and vi (which can be used in place of I). Pre-dominant chords are ones that come before dominant (ii, IV). Dominant chords are that lead back to tonic – the V and the leading tone triad, the viio. Both of the dominant chords (the V7 and viio) contain a the tritone (4-7 scale degrees) leads to 3-1, which are part of the tonic chords.

> The above progression shows the down a 5th progression (ii V I) as PD-D-T.

> The III chord is a tricky chord. Since it shares 2 notes with I, it can be used as tonic. But it also shares 2 notes with V. Especially when it is found in first inversion at a cadence (iii6-I) it can be thought of as a dominant substitute – a Vsub6 chord.

## Primary claims (one-liners)
- T-PD-D grouping emerges naturally from circle-of-fifths voice-leading (ii→V→I as PD-D-T).
- Dominant function is acoustically tied to tritone (4–7) resolving to tonic tones (3–1).
- vi substitutes for I (including deceptive cadences); iii can function as tonic extension or dominant substitute.
- viio6 first inversion retains predominant/dominant function while minimizing tritone harshness.

## Relevance to charter Q1
Voice-leading rationale for why PD→D→T progressions sound inevitable—supports ear training to hear tritone tension and predict tonic arrival in accompaniment contexts.
