# [SRC-016]: Bruce Arnold — Contextual Ear Training (Key Center vs. Intervals)
**URL**: https://brucearnold.com/ear-training-guided-tour/
**Captured**: 2026-06-14
**Type**: Pedagogical method / course overview

## Verbatim snippets / quotes
> "We have found that the most popular ear training method i.e. the use of 'intervals' will not help you to develop the skills you need as an improvising musician. Instead, we have found that an approach we call Contextual Ear Training will help you develop the skill to hear pitches. This method is based on hearing notes in relation to a 'Key Center.'"

> "Contextual Ear Training is usually done with its sister course Ear Training One Note Complete. The combination of both courses gives a student a listening and singing regimen of exercises that will totally transform your ability to hear music. Both courses are not based on interval training or the distance between notes but upon the sound of each pitch within a key center."

> "When they sing the 3rd they are not thinking that the note is four half steps above the root or in relation to any other note — they are recognizing the sound of a 3rd because they have memorized that sound within a 'key center.'"

> "Once you are getting around 80% with the 'Contextual Ear Training' you can move on to the second singing book Fanatic's Guide to Sight Singing and Ear Training. You could also add in the 'Secondary Dominants' book. This book shows you how to hear chord progressions in one key center which is essential to understanding how you should think and hear music when you are playing."

## Primary claims
- Interval-based ear training is widely taught but insufficient for improvising musicians who need to hear in real musical context.
- Contextual Ear Training trains recognition of all 12 pitches relative to a key center, not as distances from other notes.
- Mastery threshold for progression is ~80% accuracy on contextual one-note exercises before advancing to sight-singing and secondary-dominant chord-progression work.
- The system is sequential: single-note key-center recognition → two-note/modulation → chord progressions → melodic hearing.
- Singing exercises are central; recognition should become instantaneous ("like seeing the color blue").

## Relevance to charter Q2
Directly supports Q2's goal of hearing chord quality and progressions in tonal context rather than as abstract interval math. Arnold's key-center model aligns with functional hearing of major/minor/7th colors within a key — a prerequisite for improvising over changes and identifying tom by ear.
