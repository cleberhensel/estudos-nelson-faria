# [SRC-015]: Functional harmony ear training (rec.music.theory discussion)

**Type**: article

## Verbatim snippets / quotes
> Basically, all tones in a progression are sung and heard in relation to the tonic of the key.

> Functional harmony is more about [...] recognized from what happened and not from [...] You can't determine function from the chord you are hearing at that moment because you haven't heard where it is going. Hence you have to determine function from where the chord came from.

> If your in some song in C and you hear a G7 chord YOU CANNOT SAY IT IS V7 in C!!! Now you might expect it to be V7 in C and thats fine. Cadences tend to help solidify function. So if the song cadences in C after that chord then you can safely say it is a V7 in C.

> In some sense functional analysis is telling us what is going to happen while we heard what has happened. You cannot functionally analyze the current chord because you don't know what is going to happen.

> The performer can anticipate the modulation. The performer can also negate a modulation (keep it as a tonicization). Both of these things can be more or less difficult depending on how closely related the keys are. Closely related keys favor tonicization.

## Primary claims (one-liners)
- Functional hearing is retrospective/contextual—a chord's role depends on what preceded it and what follows.
- G7 in C is probabilistically V7 but ambiguous until resolution confirms (cadence solidifies function).
- Performers predict forward; analysts label backward—real-time accompaniment requires both modes.
- Tonicization vs modulation distinction depends on key relatedness and performer intent.

## Relevance to charter Q1
Critical nuance for charter Q1: chord prediction from key is probabilistic and context-dependent, not label-by-snapshot—function emerges from progression memory and expected cadential outcomes during live accompaniment.
