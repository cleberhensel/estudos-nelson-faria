# [SRC-024]: Listening for Diminished Triads — Three Recognition Methods
**URL**: https://www.musical-u.com/learn/listening-for-diminished-triads/
**Captured**: 2026-06-14
**Type**: Pedagogical article / practice guide

## Verbatim snippets / quotes
> "A diminished chord is a chord that is built by placing two minor third intervals on top of each other."

> "Drummers, singers, and other musicians who do not play harmonic instruments like the piano may have a tougher time hearing diminished chords. However, by training your ear, you'll be able to acquire this skill in no time!"

> "There are a few different ways to recognize diminished chords in popular music. Depending on your musical training, you might find one method is easier than another method, or you might use a combination of methods."

> "1. By Rote Memorization — You listen to the chord over and over again until you have essentially memorized how the chord sounds in comparison to other chords."

> "2. By Arpeggiation — break up the chord into its individual notes and essentially sing the intervals… Recognize the minor third intervals."

> "3. By Chord Quality — how the chord sounds in terms of feeling, tone, and even color."

> "Diminished triads often function as passing tones or are added for extra color… Typically, they only occur for a single beat or two within the verse or chorus of a song."

## Primary claims
- Diminished recognition is harder for non-harmonic instrumentalists but trainable via multiple parallel methods.
- Rote comparison (dim vs. maj/min in alternation) is the simplest entry point.
- Arpeggiation reveals stacked minor thirds as structural fingerprint.
- Chord-quality method (tense, quirky, "jazzy" vs. morose minor) works when interval skills are weak.
- Diminished chords in pop are brief passing/color tones, not prolonged progression pillars.
- Dim7 adds another minor third; quality remains similar to dim triad for hearing purposes.
