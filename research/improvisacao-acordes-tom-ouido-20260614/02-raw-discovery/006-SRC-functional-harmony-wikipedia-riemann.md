## Verbatim snippets / quotes
> The German theory created by Hugo Riemann in his Vereinfachte Harmonielehre of 1893 [...] is the theory of functions properly speaking. Riemann identified three abstract tonal "functions"— tonic, dominant and subdominant—denoted by the letters T, D, and S, respectively, each of which could take on a more or less modified appearance in any chord of the scale.

> The term "functional harmony" derives from Riemann and particularly from his Harmony Simplified. Riemann's direct inspiration was Moritz Hauptmann's dialectic description of tonality. Riemann identified three abstract functions: the tonic, the dominant (its upper fifth), and the subdominant (its lower fifth).

> Diether de la Motte's [...] the three tonal functions [...] can in principle be represented by three chords: the main chord corresponding to the function and the chords [...] a third apart, as indicated by additional letters. An additional letter P or p indicates [...] by the relative [...] triad: for instance Tp for the minor relative of the major tonic (e.g., A minor for C major).

## Primary claims (one-liners)
- Functional harmony originates in Riemann 1893; T, D, S label abstract roles, not fixed chord shapes.
- Dominant = upper fifth of tonic; subdominant = lower fifth—symmetric tonal hierarchy.
- Parallel (P/p) and Gegenklang (G/g) suffixes extend each function to relative and counter-relative chords.
- Theory remains standard in German and Northern/Eastern European harmony pedagogy.
