# [SRC-027]: Kodály Method — Solfege, Inner Ear & Harmonic Progression Hearing
**URL**: https://ebrary.net/170596/education/introduction_kod_ly_method_credited_unesco_intangible_cultural_heritage
**Captured**: 2026-06-14
**Type**: Pedagogical method overview / UNESCO heritage documentation

## Verbatim snippets / quotes
> "Along with the learning of the pentatonic scale degrees and rhythmic placements, Kodaly simultaneously encouraged students to develop an 'inner ear': the ability to hear music in the mind."

> "In order to do this he used solfege and hand signs to represent each pitch within the scale."

> "After teaching single-line melodies using signs, the teacher can progress to signing two notes simultaneously, developing the ability to sight sing in harmony."

> "Using solfege to sing the roots of chord progressions develops a greater awareness and understanding of harmonic progression, which can lead to more sensitive and expressive performances that reveal in-depth understanding."

> "More formal vocal training can come from Kodaly's Let Us Sing Correctly, which includes traditional notation and starts with drones, singing notes of the tonic chord against this, and so on."

## Primary claims
- Kodály builds hearing through moveable-do solfege + Curwen hand signs before notation.
- Inner ear (audiation) is an explicit developmental goal, not a byproduct.
- Two-part solfege signing bridges melody hearing to harmonic awareness.
- Singing chord roots in solfege develops progression comprehension for all instrumentalists.
- Drone + tonic-chord singing is the harmonic entry point in formal Kodály choral training.
- Kinesthetic hand-sign spatialization reinforces pitch relationships.

## Relevance to charter Q2
Offers an alternative/complementary path to interval-only or key-center-only methods: functional solfege for scale-degree and root-progression hearing — valuable for vocalists in repertório work and for connecting chord quality to tonal function.
